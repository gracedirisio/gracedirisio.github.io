Current Work

I study how neural codes support visual perception and consequently guide behavior. My research combines psychophysics, in vivo electrophysiology, and computational work.

These interests developed while I worked as a technician at the University of Rochester (DeAngelis lab). I mainly studied the neuronal basis of a cue in optic flow, termed the "dynamic perspective cue," that occurs during natural self-movements. This cue is used by our visual system to guide depth perception.

I came to graduate school to merge my interests in vision with work in cognition. I am currently doing so at the University of Chicago as a fourth year PhD candidate working in the Cohen lab. Through recording from populations of neurons and developing a novel task paradigm, I aim to parse out characteristics of the neural code during visual processing that aid image recognition memory. Humans can remember thousands of images after a single exposure — a remarkable capability that remains poorly understood at the neuronal level. My work will uncover how the brain achieves this, and in turn will provide new insight on memory disorders and help improve artificial systems.
